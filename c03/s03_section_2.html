Angkas launched motorcycle taxis in a country where they were illegal. Commuters had many pains: fares, availability, hours lost in traffic, safety. Which ONE pain did Angkas build the whole company around?

Safety. Speed was the obvious sell (that's why commuters ride), but safety was the pain that decided everything: it was why passengers hesitated, why families objected, and why regulators kept the service illegal. Rank the pains honestly and the top one is not always the loudest one. The section below gives you the math for that ranking.
Field notes · a real founder
Angeline Tham · Angkas · Manila, 2016 to 2019

In 2016, Angeline Tham spent six hours crossing Manila in a single day. She founded Angkas that year to put commuters on motorcycle taxis, with fares starting near ₱50, in a market where a decades-old law made the service illegal. The pain list was long: cost, availability, hours lost in traffic. Tham ranked one pain above all of them: safety. It was the pain passengers felt, the objection families raised, and the reason regulators said no.

So the offer was engineered against that single pain. Applicants took a written exam and a driving course on top of the government license, trained free in Angkas centers, and rode with helmets and insurance. Bloomberg reported that only 1 in 4 applicants passed. The company kept a safety record it reports at 99.997%. When regulators suspended the service, that safety case, backed by public outcry, helped win a government pilot program in 2019. By 2023 Angkas had given free training to around 200,000 riders.

Frequency times severity made safety the top-ranked pain, and every reliever in the stack (vetting, training, the pass bar, insurance) points at it. That is what ranking discipline looks like when a company bets on it.
Sources: Bloomberg via BusinessWorld (2019) · PNA (2020) · Inquirer (2023)

Your interview notes hold more pains than you can serve. An unranked list is functionally useless: it says what the customer cares about in some sense, but not what they care about most. The ranking heuristic is three questions, each scored 1 to 5.

Frequency. How often does the pain bite? Every weekday beats twice a year. Severity. How hard does it bite? A ruined day beats a mild annoyance. Addressability. Can you credibly relieve it with your team, money, and timeline? A pain you cannot address scores low no matter how real it is; it is simply not your pain to solve.

The same math works for gains, with severity read as the size of the positive outcome. Score, sort, keep the top three, and write the rest down somewhere you can ignore them with a clear conscience. Discipline here is the difference between a product and a feature soup.
