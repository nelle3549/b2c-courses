Fit you can count, wording they can recognize.

Two propositions for the same bookkeeping app. Which converts better with stall owners?

The second, and every stall owner you interviewed could tell you why: it is their Friday night, their receipts, their shop, in their words. The first has all the right structural pieces and zero customer vocabulary, so nobody recognizes their own life in it. This section gives you the test that catches it.
Field notes · real founders
Roland Ros & Rexy Dorado · Kumu · Manila, 2017 to 2021

Kumu began in 2017 as a messenger app for Filipinos at home and abroad. Out of the first few thousand downloads, Dorado has said, only twenty or thirty people stuck around, and nearly all of them were using one feature: the livestream. The founders followed that signal and rebuilt around live community.

Then they named the whole proposition in the customer's own language. The app is called Kumu, from "Kumusta ka?", the greeting every Filipino reaches for first. The promised gain was never "interactive streaming technology"; it was connection, a way for the diaspora to feel at home. That wording carried: by 2021 Kumu counted over 10 million registered users in more than 55 countries and closed a US$73.5M Series C, bringing total funding past US$100M, billed at the time as the largest round ever for a Philippine startup outside the conglomerates.

The proposition is literally written in the customer's first word. That is the wording test passed at company scale: customers recognized themselves before anyone explained a feature.
Sources: Kumu (about) · Rappler · Business Wire (2021)

Your canvas is nearly full. Two checks remain before the proposition is ready, and both are mechanical.

The fit check is counting. Three top pains, three relievers, three lines. Three top gains, matching creators, lines again. If an item on the value side points at nothing ranked, it floats, and it waits. If a top-ranked pain has no reliever, the canvas is not done. Fit is structural; you can audit it in thirty seconds.

The wording test is reading aloud. Read your proposition and count whose words it uses. If the vocabulary is yours (platform, optimize, seamless, empower), you have written a feature list in a costume. If the vocabulary is theirs (Friday night, resibo, close the shop, Saturday with the kids), the customer hears their own life read back to them. Polish is not what converts; recognition is.

Worked example · the same product, two propositions

✗ Founder words "Our enterprise-grade AI-powered platform leverages advanced algorithms to optimize your workflow." Customer words used: zero. Every noun came from a pitch deck. It cannot land because nobody lives in it.

✓ Their words "Stop spending Friday night reconciling supplier receipts. We close your books before you close the shop." The hated moment (Friday night, receipts) and the wanted moment (books closed before the shop closes), both taken straight from interviews. Same product, same fit, different language, and only one of them gets forwarded to a kumare.

The test in one line: if the customer would not say the words back to you, you have not done the wording work yet.
