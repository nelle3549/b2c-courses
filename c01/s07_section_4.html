The map of the PH ecosystem, and the door with your name on it.

A third-year engineering student has a working thesis prototype and needs ₱500,000 to pilot it. No company, no revenue, no investor contacts. Where does that money realistically come from?

The grant pipe. Banks need collateral she doesn't have; VCs need traction she doesn't have yet. But RA 11337 created Startup Grant Funds under DOST, DICT, and DTI for exactly her case, and the university Technology Business Incubator (TBI) exists to walk her application into them. This section maps that machinery.

Section 1 gave you the legal definition of a startup. Here is what that definition unlocks. Since 2019, RA 11337 (the Innovative Startup Act) has authorized real benefits for ventures that fit it: subsidies for registration and permit fees, subsidized office space and facilities, grants-in-aid for research and pilots, a Startup Grant Fund under each of DOST, DICT, and DTI, a Startup Venture Fund that matches investor money, and even startup visas for foreign co-founders. The catch is that none of it comes to you. You walk to it, through an ecosystem that looks chaotic from the outside and sorts into four layers from the inside.

Layer 3 is closer than most students think: DOST-PCIEERD funds Technology Business Incubators inside universities across the country (UP and Ateneo in Luzon, USC and UPV in the Visayas, USTP and MSU-IIT in Mindanao, among others), and private hubs like QBO and IdeaSpace run cohorts open to anyone with a sharp problem statement. The sequencing mistake that filters out most first-time applicants is skipping straight to Layer 2: a grant application written by someone who has never sat in a Layer 3 room reads exactly like what it is.

Field notes · real founders
JT Solis, Jeff Barreiro & co-founders · Mayani · 2019–2023

Mayani started in 2019 with a flat-footed, unglamorous problem: smallholder farmers and fisherfolk selling into long chains of middlemen, keeping little of the final price. The five co-founders built a marketplace bridging that fragmented supply to recurring institutional demand: hotels, food processors, supermarkets. A real problem and a working model, but in 2021 they were still a small team with a big map to cross.

Their move was a Layer 3 door: they joined AgFunder's Grow Impact Accelerator in 2021. The cohort shaped the model and the network vouched for the team, and when the seed round came together, it was led by the same fund that ran the accelerator: US$1.7 million anchored by AgFunder's Grow Impact Fund, joined by IdeaSpace, Plug and Play, and angels including the Jimenez family. By their count, around 139,000 farmers and fisherfolk and some 500 institutional buyers now trade through the platform.

The right door compounds. Mayani didn't cold-pitch capital; they entered a room (L3) where capital could watch them work, and the room became the round. That is what "capital is the result" looks like in practice.

Worked example · four founder positions, four first doors
You are...Your first door
A student with a thesis or org-project prototypeYour campus TBI, and through it the DOST grant pipeline. Cost: time, not equity.
An employee with a problem you've complained about for yearsAn accelerator cohort (QBO, IdeaSpace): the schedule forces the leap, and capital follows the cohort.
A mid-career domain expert with an insider problemThe Startup Grant Fund directly: your career pre-validates the problem statement. Write it sharp.
A returning OFW with savings and instinctsCommunity first (L4), then interviews: your assumptions need local ground truth before any application.

Readiness is not a feeling; it's a door. Every founder position above is ready for one specific door right now. The wrong door is the one that requires you to pretend to be a different founder than you are.
