The three marks, part one: born as a project, run on a non-obvious idea.

Before it was a company worth trillions, Google began as...

A research project. Larry Page and Sergey Brin were Stanford PhD students studying how web pages link to each other; the company came two years after the experiment. Hold that order: experiment first, company later. It repeats in almost every startup you've heard of, including the Filipino ones below.

A startup carries three marks. This section covers the first two; the next section stress-tests the third, because it's the one founders most often get wrong.

Mark 1 · Initiated as a project. Startups begin as experimental ventures: a trial, a pilot, a thing built to find something out. Not a corporation, not a business plan, not a launch party. The pattern became famous during the dot-com years and it has never stopped. Look at the birth certificates:

The Philippine rows matter most here. GCash started in 2004 as an SMS money-transfer experiment inside Globe, built for the majority of Filipinos who had no bank account, alongside Smart's rival experiment, Smart Padala. No smartphone apps, no QR codes: text messages moving money at the speed and cost of a text. The experiments worked, and two decades later one of them sits in almost every Filipino phone.

Mark 2 · An innovative solution. A startup attacks a real problem with a non-obvious answer: high risk, high reward. Obvious answers (another laundry shop, another reselling page) already have owners; that's why they're safe and crowded. And note what RA 11337 counts as innovation: a new or improved product, process, or business model. The law's own words. The innovation in GCash was never a gadget; it was a process (money over SMS) and a business model (cash-in through the sari-sari network). You do not need a laboratory to qualify.

Worked example · invention is not commercialization
InventionCommercialization
A technical solution to a technical problem.The process of deriving income from a technology.
The working prototype: the matching algorithm, the solar dryer, the saltwater lamp.The prototype manufactured, priced, distributed, supported, and paid for, again and again.
Ends with "it works."Ends with "they keep paying."

A startup is the bridge between the two columns. Grant panels and investors fund the bridge, not the trophy: a brilliant invention with no path to income is a science-fair entry, and a modest process innovation that people pay for weekly is a business.

Field notes · real founders
Bing Tan, Ibba Bernardo & Hubert Yap · Packworks · 2018–2022

The three Packworks founders are motorcycle riders who used their long provincial rides to bring solar panels to far-flung communities. On those trips they kept resting at the same place: the neighborhood sari-sari store. And they kept seeing the same thing. Store owners tracking inventory by memory and notebook, struggling to get supplies, paying more the further from the city they stood.

In 2018 they started Packworks as a project with exactly five partner stores: a pilot to test one non-obvious idea, that the country's smallest retailers could run on a real business tool if it was built for low-bandwidth phones and sachet-sized workflows. The experiment repeated. By 2022, without raising outside money, the platform had grown from those five stores to 150,000 by the company's count, and the founders closed a US$2 million seed round led by Fast Group and CVC Capital Partners.

Both marks in one arc: initiated as a project (five stores, one hypothesis) and an innovative solution that wasn't obvious (nobody was building enterprise software for sari-sari stores). The scale came later, and that's the next section.
