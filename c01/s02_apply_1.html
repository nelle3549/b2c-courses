Startup, or not a startup?

Use the test: does the next customer cost less to serve than the last? Tap one answer per row.

A milk-tea shop opening its third branch in Iligan, each with its own staff and stock.

Not a startup: it's a small business becoming an SME. Each new branch carries its own rent, staff, and inventory, so the next customer costs about the same as the last. A fine business; a different animal.

A two-person team testing an app that matches probinsya farmers directly to city buyers.

Startup: still tiny, still unproven, but it's searching for a repeatable model. Once the matching engine works, farmer 5,000 costs almost nothing more to serve than farmer 50.

A freelance web-design studio taking on more client projects every month.

Not a startup: every new client needs new hours. Services businesses grow with headcount, so the cost per customer stays flat. (It can become a startup if the team turns its service into a product.)

A solo developer with no company yet, piloting a jeepney-fare wallet with one route's drivers.

Startup, even without papers: RA 11337's definition starts at "any person... which aims to develop an innovative product, process, or business model." The pilot is the search; the wallet repeats route by route.
