Section 3

The third mark: growing is not scaling.

Your turnPredictTap an answer

A milk-tea brand doubles its revenue by opening a second branch. What most likely happened to its costs?

They nearly doubled. A second branch is a second copy of every cost: rent, staff, stock, utilities. Revenue went up, so the business grew. But nothing got cheaper per customer, so it did not scale. That difference is the whole section, and it's the mark investors and grant panels check hardest.

The deck version of this mark reads: poised for rapid growth, able to reach many people and generate revenue, usually riding technology with low market-entry costs. Useful, but the working test is sharper. Watch what the costs do:

Growing means revenue rises because you keep buying the things that produce it: another branch, another hire, another van. Revenue and costs climb the same hill together, and every push needs new investment. Scaling means revenue rises while costs stay nearly flat, because the engine was built once and each new customer is another cheap copy. One curve pulls away from the other.

The world's scoreboard already reflects this. In 2011, the ten most valuable companies on Earth were mostly oil, mining, and banking businesses; only two were technology companies. By 2021 the list had flipped: eight of the ten were technology platforms. Oil rigs and bank branches grow; software scales, because the next copy costs almost nothing to serve.

Field notes · real founders
JC Bisnar, JM Lapiña & Airwyn Tin · Investagrams · Taguig, 2015–2019

Three students who learned stock trading in college kept meeting the same wall: fewer than one in a hundred Filipinos invested in the market, and no platform existed to teach the rest. In 2015 they built Investagrams, a free social platform with charts, virtual trading, and lessons in plain language. They had almost no capital, so they refused every cost that didn't scale. No hires: they did everything themselves. No ad budget: they partnered with a social network for free credits. No salaries: earnings went back into the product.

The engine they built once kept serving new users at almost no new cost. By late 2016 the platform counted 50,000 users and about two million views a month; by 2018, more than 200,000 users; by 2019, over 360,000. The team stayed a handful of people. Costs stayed nearly flat while the user curve climbed away from them: the scaling chart above, drawn in real life.

Scalability isn't a funding trick; it's a design property. Investagrams could scale on pocket money because the product, not the payroll, did the serving. If your idea can only grow by hiring, it will grow like a branch business, whatever app it ships with.
