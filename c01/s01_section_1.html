Field notes · real founders
Ron Hose & Runar Petursson · Coins.ph · Manila, 2013–2019

In 2013, Ron Hose spent a week in every market in Southeast Asia looking for the right place to build. He chose Manila, where up to 70 percent of Filipinos had no bank account, and queues for bills and remittances ate whole mornings. The obvious response was a bank's response: put up branches. Hose and co-founder Runar Petursson built the opposite: Coins.ph, launched in 2014, put the financial service inside the phone people already owned.

That one design choice is the working definition of a startup in action. A branch serves the block it stands on; an app serves whoever downloads it. Riding partner networks like 7-Eleven for cash-in and cash-out, Coins.ph reached 17,000 physical touchpoints without building one of them, and by 2018 it counted around five million customers. In January 2019, Indonesia's Gojek bought a majority stake for about US$72 million.

Coins.ph was never "a small bank that grew." It was a different animal from day one: a team searching for a repeatable, scalable model, then repeating it. That search is what the word "startup" actually names.
Sources: GoodNewsPilipinas · Go Negosyo (2018) · TechCrunch (2019) · Endeavor

The working definition, from startup teacher Steve Blank: a startup is a temporary organization designed to search for a repeatable and scalable business model. Every word is load-bearing. Temporary: the startup phase ends once the model is found (Coins.ph in 2019 was no longer searching). Search: the model is not known yet; the company exists to find it. Repeatable: serving customer 1,001 works the same as serving customer 1. Scalable: each repeat costs less than the one before.

The second definition is the one written into Philippine law. Republic Act No. 11337, the Innovative Startup Act of 2019, defines a startup as "any person or registered entity in the Philippines which aims to develop an innovative product, process, or business model." Notice how wide that is: you don't need a company yet (any person qualifies), and the innovation can live in the product, the process, or the business model. This is the definition a DOST or DTI grant panel applies when your application lands on their desk, and the law puts real money behind it: grant funds, registration subsidies, even startup visas. Section 4 walks that map.

One more line worth keeping, because it saves you from a common trap: not all new or small companies are startups, but most startups start small. Size and age are how the three venture types get confused. The growth model is how they come apart.

Worked example · one laundry idea, three animals
As a...The venture looks like
Small businessA laundry shop by the church. The owner knows every customer. Growth means longer hours.
SMEEight branches across the city, a manager in each, a delivery van. Growth means branch nine.
StartupAn app matching idle washing machines to nearby customers. Growth means the next city, at almost no added cost.

All three are legitimate. Only one is designed to repeat. Which one you're building decides which door you knock on later, so answer it honestly.
