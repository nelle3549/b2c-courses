You get one question with a sari-sari store owner. Which produces truer data?

The second one, and it's not close. The first asks her to predict her future self while being polite to your face; you'll get a "sige, siguro" that means nothing. The second asks her to replay something that actually happened. She can't be politely wrong about what she did: she either texted the supplier, sent her anak to the bodega, or lost the sale. That's data.
Field notes · a real founder
ER Rollan · GrowSari · Metro Manila, 2016

Reymund "ER" Rollan had spent years at P&G and BCG studying the mom-and-pop retail channel from boardrooms. When he and three co-founders launched GrowSari in 2016, a B2B platform for sari-sari stores, they didn't start with a survey. For the first 100 stores, the founders went store-to-store themselves: pitched at the counter, took the orders, did the grocery runs, packed the boxes, drove the deliveries.

Many tinderas refused. Some suspected a scam. Those refusals were the point. The objections, workarounds, and counter-top habits they observed became the design of the platform, one that by 2022 served 100,000+ stores across 220 municipalities and raised a $77.5M round. Harvard Business School now teaches the case.

Rollan wasn't doing logistics those first months. He was doing empathy work: placing himself where user behavior could be observed, not just asked about. Empathy here is a verb.

The empathy phase is not about being kind. It is about positioning yourself so the data you collect is behavioral, not self-reported. Every data point you gather sits somewhere on a ladder. The higher the level, the more you can trust it:

The empathy interview reaches level 3 by asking about a specific recent moment: "Tell me about the last time you needed a tutor. Walk me through what happened." Then follow the story downhill: "What did you try before that?" "What was the most annoying part?" "Did you ask anyone for help?" You never mention your product. You are asking about that one Saturday, and ten of those Saturdays will tell you more than a thousand survey rows.

The observation reaches level 4: watch the user do the thing, without narrating or suggesting. Only afterward, ask them to replay their thinking. The gap between what they did and what they say they did is itself data: it shows you which parts of the experience are conscious and which are habit.
