Section 3

Reframing: from symptom to problem.

Your turnPredictTap an answer

A user tells you: "Ang bagal ng app niyo." What's your first move?

Ask first. "Slow" is a symptom: a true sentence pointing at something it doesn't name. The performance sprint, the animation, and the discount all treat the sentence. The question treats the situation: last Tuesday, 6 p.m., MRT signal, trying to order dinner before the station. Same five words, completely different problem.
Field notes · a real founder
Patrick Lynch · First Circle · Manila, 2016–2018

When Patrick Lynch launched First Circle in 2016, the complaint from Philippine SMEs was unanimous: "we need a loan." Banks took a month to say no; for 80% of First Circle's early customers, this was their first formal relationship with any financial institution. So First Circle did the obvious: fast, unsecured working-capital loans, first to Lazada and Zalora sellers.

Then the loan data started talking. Borrowers weren't broke. Their money existed, it was just trapped: big buyers were paying invoices in 90 to 120 days. The complaint said loan. The problem was locked receivables. In 2018 First Circle rebuilt around it: purchase-order and invoice financing, funding the wait itself. By 2026 it had disbursed over ₱25 billion to 5,000+ SMEs and marked its tenth year.

The move Lynch's team made has a name, reframing: refusing to accept the stated complaint as the problem, and digging until the situation underneath it shows its structure.
Sources: TechCrunch (2018) · Inquirer (2017) · First Circle (2026)

This is the single highest-leverage move in design thinking. The symptom is what the user says; the underlying problem is what the situation is. Users are excellent witnesses and unreliable diagnosticians: they experience the pain precisely and locate its cause loosely.

Two tools take you under the waterline, and they sometimes disagree. The 5 Whys is mechanical and fast: ask why five times, anchoring each answer in something the user said or did. Its failure mode is drift: one unanchored why and the chain leads somewhere arbitrary. Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD) asks instead: what is this user trying to accomplish in this situation? Slower, but harder to derail, because it stays anchored to the user's outcome. When the two tools disagree, trust JTBD. Usually the 5 Whys has drifted.

Worked example · "The grocery app is too slow," dug twice
  1. Why slow? Loading takes forever. (anchored: she showed the spinner)
  2. Why long loads? Connection keeps dropping. (anchored: phone status bar in her screen recording)
  3. Why dropping? She shops during her commute. (anchored: "pag-uwi ko, sa MRT")
  4. Why then? It's her only free 20 minutes to plan dinner. (anchored: her schedule, not our guess)
  5. Why does that break the app? The app assumes a stable connection she never has at that hour.
  6. Root: the architecture assumes a context the user doesn't live in: no offline mode fits her actual shopping window.

JTBD check: the job is "plan dinner during a 20-minute commute on a bad connection." The app's real competitor isn't another grocery app. It's a napkin list. Both tools land on the same root here. When they don't, re-anchor the whys.
