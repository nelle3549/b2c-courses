Healthy: about five or six directions surface (voice logging, photo-of-list capture, SMS ledger, customer self-record, QR tabs, auto-reminders), and every one stays anchored to the same user and job. That 60-second count is the whole test, and you'll use it on your own HMW on the next page.
Field notes · real founders
Francis Plaza, Jaime Hing, Luis Sia, Edwin Lacierda · PayMongo · Manila, 2019

The four PayMongo founders knew the pain from inside: Filipino online sellers ran payments on bank deposits and photographed deposit slips, and businesses that wanted card payments waited months for gateway approvals. "It takes months, we tried it ourselves," Plaza told TechCrunch. By mid-2019 they were the first Philippine fintech in Y Combinator, with a product they kept polishing instead of releasing.

The advice that unstuck them, Lacierda later wrote, was two words from a YC mentor. In June 2019 they went live with exactly ten merchants. That launch was a bet that could fail in plain sight: if the pain was real, usage would grow without pushing. Transactions grew 117% week over week on average; by late September, 1,000+ businesses were on the platform and the founders closed a $2.7M seed round backed by Stripe and Peter Thiel.

Ten merchants and one growth metric made PayMongo's problem statement falsifiable: the world could prove it wrong within weeks, cheaply. That is the standard your problem statement meets today, on paper, before any launch.

You now hold an underlying problem (Field 3). Two moves turn it into something a team can act on this week: frame it as a How Might We, and sharpen it into a falsifiable statement.

A well-formed HMW is the contract between your empathy work and your ideation. The test is the one you just ran: read the HMW, count distinct solution directions in 60 seconds. 4–8 is healthy. Under 4, you've smuggled in a solution. Over 12, you've written a wish.

Worked example · five HMWs, graded

✓ Healthy "How might we help commuting parents plan dinner in under three minutes on a degraded connection?" about 6 directions: preloaded menus, voice input, offline mode, templates, auto-ordering, time-boxed UX. One user, one job, one context.

✓ Healthy "How might we let SME owners reconcile weekly supplier receipts in under 15 minutes?" about 5 directions: OCR, supplier-side entry, batch matching, bank integrations, simplified flow. Note the measurable "under 15 minutes."

✓ Healthy "How might we let returning OFWs identify a local logistics co-founder within 90 days?" about 5 directions: matchmaking, community events, mentor pipelines, screening frameworks, paid intros.

✗ Too narrow "How might we build a green button on the checkout page?" one direction only. The solution already got designed; ideation is theater from here.

✗ Too vague "How might we eliminate all friction in onboarding?" "all" unbounds the scope; twenty directions, none anchored. The team will scatter.

The HMW opens the design space. The falsifiable problem statement is its evidence twin: the version a weekend could prove wrong. It names who (a specific segment), what (an observable behavior or pain), how much (a frequency or severity you'd expect to see), and is checkable within 7 days. "Filipinos need better financial tools" can never be wrong, which is exactly what's wrong with it. "Cebu City SME owners who pay suppliers weekly spend 90+ minutes reconciling receipts every Friday, and most would call it their worst weekly task." Five interviews this week could kill that sentence. That's what makes it worth writing.
