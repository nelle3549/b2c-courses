Most failed startups didn't fail at building.

CB Insights has catalogued hundreds of startup post-mortems, written by the founders themselves. What tops the list of most-cited reasons for failure?

No market need. In the original analysis of 100+ post-mortems, 42% of founders named it, making it the single most-cited reason. Later refreshes put "ran out of cash" neck-and-neck with it, but read the post-mortems and you'll see they're usually the same disease at different stages: cash runs out because nobody needed the product enough to pay for it. If you guessed cash, team, or competition, that instinct is exactly why this section exists.
Field notes · a real founder
Paul Rivera · Kalibrr · Manila, 2012–2013

In 2012, Paul Rivera was sure he knew the problem: Filipino applicants kept failing BPO interviews, so Kalibrr built an online training platform (English, technical skills, test prep) and signed up outsourcing firms to pay for graduates. The idea was strong enough to make Kalibrr the first Philippine startup accepted into Y Combinator.

Less than a year later, the training model wasn't holding. In May 2013 Rivera shut it down and let ten people go, friends who had started the company with him. That December, Kalibrr relaunched as a job-matching platform, the product employers and jobseekers actually pulled toward, with ₱83 million in fresh backing.

What Rivera paid (a year, ten teammates, and the company he first imagined) is the standard tuition of solution-first building. The discipline below, problem-first thinking, is how you pay for the same lesson in weeks instead.
Sources: Rappler (2014) · YugaTech (2012) · Philstar (2013)

Kalibrr's arc is the CB Insights dataset in miniature. The team built competently. The training platform worked. What was missing wasn't execution; it was a problem the payer felt strongly enough to keep paying for. No market need doesn't mean "bad idea." It means the team's build order was backwards: solution first, evidence later.

The discipline this course teaches is what happens before shipping. The habit is mechanical: run empathy interviews instead of feature meetings. Watch users do the thing instead of asking how they feel about it. Reframe complaints into the underlying problem. Write a problem statement sharp enough that next weekend could prove you wrong.

Worked example · the same founder brain, two ways
Solution-first saysProblem-first asks
"People will love this.""Who has this pain, and what did they do about it last week?"
"We just need to explain the features better.""What is the user already paying, in time, money, or lakad, to work around this?"
"Let's add more features para sulit.""Which single pain, if removed, would they notice within a day?"
"Nobody's using it yet kasi mahina ang marketing.""Did anyone change behavior when we removed the pain by hand, even once?"

Notice the pattern: every problem-first question can be answered with evidence this week. No solution-first statement can.
